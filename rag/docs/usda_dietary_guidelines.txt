USDA Dietary Guidelines — Nutrition Reference (Stub)

== Protein ==

Protein is an essential macronutrient required for building and repairing tissues, producing enzymes and hormones, and supporting immune function. The Dietary Reference Intake (DRI) for protein is 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight per day for sedentary adults. However, individuals who are physically active, particularly those engaged in resistance training or endurance exercise, may benefit from higher intakes in the range of 1.2 to 2.0 grams per kilogram of body weight. High-quality protein sources include lean meats, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy products, legumes, tofu, and tempeh. Distributing protein intake evenly across meals — aiming for 25–40 grams per meal — supports muscle protein synthesis more effectively than consuming the majority of protein in a single meal. Older adults are advised to target the higher end of the protein range to counteract age-related muscle loss (sarcopenia).

== Carbohydrates ==

Carbohydrates are the body's primary and preferred source of energy, particularly for the brain and during high-intensity exercise. The USDA recommends that carbohydrates make up 45–65% of total daily caloric intake. The emphasis should be placed on complex carbohydrates — such as whole grains, legumes, vegetables, and fruits — rather than refined carbohydrates and added sugars. Added sugars should be limited to less than 10% of total daily calories. The glycemic index (GI) of foods influences how quickly blood glucose rises after eating; low-GI foods such as oats, lentils, and most non-starchy vegetables promote more stable blood sugar levels. Adequate carbohydrate intake is especially important for athletes and highly active individuals who rely on glycogen stores for sustained performance.

== Dietary Fats ==

Dietary fats are essential for absorbing fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, and K), supporting cell membrane integrity, and providing a concentrated source of energy at 9 calories per gram. Total fat intake should represent 20–35% of total daily calories. Unsaturated fats — including monounsaturated fats found in olive oil and avocados, and polyunsaturated fats found in fatty fish, walnuts, and flaxseed — are associated with reduced risk of cardiovascular disease and should comprise the majority of fat intake. Saturated fat should be limited to less than 10% of total calories, and trans fats should be avoided entirely. Omega-3 fatty acids, specifically EPA and DHA found in fatty fish such as salmon, mackerel, and sardines, play an important role in reducing inflammation and supporting heart and brain health. Adults are encouraged to consume at least two servings of fatty fish per week.

== Dietary Fiber ==

Dietary fiber is a type of carbohydrate that the body cannot digest, yet it plays a critical role in digestive health, blood sugar regulation, and cholesterol management. The adequate intake (AI) for fiber is 25 grams per day for adult women and 38 grams per day for adult men, though most Americans consume far less than this. Soluble fiber — found in oats, beans, apples, and psyllium — dissolves in water to form a gel-like substance that slows digestion, reduces post-meal blood glucose spikes, and helps lower LDL cholesterol. Insoluble fiber — found in whole wheat, bran, nuts, and most vegetables — adds bulk to stool and promotes regular bowel movements, reducing the risk of constipation and diverticular disease. A high-fiber diet is associated with a reduced risk of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and colorectal cancer. Increasing fiber intake gradually and maintaining adequate hydration helps prevent gastrointestinal discomfort.

== Calories and Energy Balance ==

Total caloric intake determines energy balance — the relationship between calories consumed and calories expended. The average adult requires approximately 2,000–2,500 calories per day, though individual needs vary considerably based on age, sex, body size, and physical activity level. To estimate total daily energy expenditure (TDEE), Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) — the calories burned at rest — is calculated using factors such as weight, height, age, and sex (Harris-Benedict or Mifflin-St Jeor equations are commonly used), then multiplied by an activity factor. A caloric deficit of approximately 500 calories per day is generally associated with a weight loss rate of about 0.45 kg (1 lb) per week, while a comparable surplus supports lean mass gain when combined with resistance training. Micronutrient density should not be sacrificed in pursuit of caloric targets; low-calorie diets must still provide adequate vitamins, minerals, and essential macronutrients. The USDA MyPlate framework recommends filling half the plate with fruits and vegetables, one quarter with whole grains, and one quarter with lean protein, with dairy or a dairy alternative on the side.
